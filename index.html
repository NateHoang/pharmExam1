Lecture 6 - Renal pharmacology
Drug class Example(s) Mechanism of action (MOA) Indications Key adverse effects / precautions
ACE inhibitors Captopril, Enalapril (-pril) Block angiotensin‑converting enzyme → ↓ Ang II formation → ↓ preload (blood volume) and ↓ afterload (peripheral resistance). Also ↑ bradykinin (enhances vasodilation). First‑line for hypertension in patients with heart disease, diabetes, or chronic kidney disease.
  • Dry cough, angioedema, hyperkalemia (↓ aldosterone), orthostatic hypotension, altered taste
  • contraindicated in pregnancy.
Angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs) Losartan, Valsartan Competitive antagonists at AT1 receptor → block Ang II effects (vasodilation, block aldosterone). Do not increase bradykinin. First‑line option for hypertension (esp. diabetes, HF, CKD).
  • Hypotension, hyperkalemia
  • less cough/angioedema than ACEi
  • contraindicated in pregnancy.
  • Avoid combining with ACEi.
Renin inhibitor Aliskiren (Tekturna®) Selective renin inhibitor → blocks conversion of angiotensinogen to Ang I. Hypertension.
  • May cause angioedema (less than ACEi)
  • contraindicated in pregnancy, do not combine with ACEi/ARB
  • metabolized by CYP3A4.
Loop diuretics Furosemide, Bumetanide, Torsemide Block Na+-K+-2Cl− symporter in thick ascending limb → potent natriuresis and diuresis; ↑ excretion of Na+, K+, Ca2+, Mg2+. Acute pulmonary edema, edema from renal/liver disease, chronic CHF, HTN adjunct; acute hypercalcemia (with renal failure).
  • Hypokalemia, dose‑related ototoxicity, nephrotoxicity, orthostatic hypotension, hyperuricemia, hypomagnesemia. Sigmoidal dose‑response (threshold & ceiling effects). Drug interaction: NSAIDs can blunt effect and increase nephrotoxicity.
Thiazide diuretics Chlorothiazide, Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ) Inhibit Na+/Cl− transporter in distal convoluted tubule → ↑ NaCl excretion and ↑ Ca2+ reabsorption. Also reduce peripheral vascular resistance long‑term (mechanism not fully clear). Hypertension, edema (heart/liver/kidney), prevention of calcium kidney stones, nephrogenic diabetes insipidus.
  • Hypokalemia, hyperuricemia, hypercalcemia, hyponatremia, hyperglycemia
  • cross‑allergy with sulfonamide antibiotics.
Potassium‑sparing diuretics Spironolactone (aldosterone antagonist), Amiloride Spironolactone: aldosterone receptor antagonist → ↓ Na+ reabsorption, ↓ K+ secretion. Amiloride: blocks epithelial Na+ channels (ENaC) in collecting duct. Edema, hypokalemia prevention, refractory HTN (often combined with thiazide/loop).
  • Hyperkalemia (↑ arrhythmia risk). Spironolactone may cause gynecomastia (androgen receptor effects).
Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors Acetazolamide (Diamox®) Inhibit carbonic anhydrase → reduce HCO3− formation and decrease Na+/H+ exchange → mild bicarbonate diuresis. Reduce intraocular pressure in glaucoma; prophylaxis for altitude sickness. (Overall weak diuretic.)
  • Metabolic acidosis, electrolyte disturbances (not listed in detail on deck).
Osmotic diuretics Mannitol (Osmitrol®) Filtered but not reabsorbed → increases tubular osmolarity → retains water in tubule → water diuresis. IV for cerebral edema / elevated intracranial pressure.
  • Volume shifts, electrolyte disturbances
  • use caution in heart failure/edema.
Other CVS agents (brief) Colchicine (0.5 mg/day), Aspirin (81 mg/day) Colchicine: anti‑inflammatory (used low dose for CV risk reduction). Aspirin: antiplatelet (cardioprotection). Colchicine: reduce MI/stroke risk in certain atherosclerotic patients. Aspirin: cardioprotection.
  • Aspirin: affects hemostasis (dose/interaction dependent).
  • Colchicine: GI and drug interactions (details not expanded here).
Agents with renal adverse effects (to avoid/adjust) NSAIDs, certain antimicrobials (aminoglycosides, vancomycin), some chemotherapeutics, alcohol/illicit drugs Various nephrotoxic mechanisms → can damage renal structure/function; dose adjustments required if eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m². Avoid or dose‑adjust in CKD / older patients.
  • Potential nephrotoxicity
  • monitor renal function and adjust dosing.
